Bacillus amyloliquefaciens LL3 and NK-1 [1]

Modular pathway engineering was performed on the LL3 strain of Bacillus amyloliquefaciens LL3 to optimize the biosynthetic pathway and tweak the genes corresponding to other metabolic fluxes. As shown in Fig. 1, multiple synthetic bottlenecks were manipulated in the LL3 strain to obtain Bacillus amyloliquefaciens NK-1.


Fig. 1: Modular Engineering performed on the LL3 strain to obtain NK-1. [2]

To improve γ-PGA production, the following five genetic modifications were aimed as elucidated above:

  1. By-product Reduction – Knocked out genes (eps, sac, glyc, lps, ldh, pta) to avoid any polysaccharide, lactate, and acetate production.
  2. γ-PGA Stability – Removed pgdS and cwlO genes to prevent γ-PGA degradation.
  3. Stress Tolerance – Removed luxS for better environmental protection.
  4. Improved γ-PGA Biosynthesis – Overexpressed pgsBCA via the addition of a P43 promoter.
  5. Glutamate Supply – Utilized synthetic sRNAs to down-regulate rocG and glnA, resulting in intracellular glutamate accumulation.

The engineered NK-anti-rocG strain produced γ-PGA titers of 11.04 g/L in flasks and 20.3 g/L in a 5 L fermenter, showing a 2.91-fold and 5.34-fold increase from the control strain.

However, this gene was sourced from the laboratory of Feng, et. Al., and hence not available in any of the forums such as MTCC, ATCC or NCIMB. Hence, it was decided to proceed with a different strain.

A study has also shown that gamma pga is produced in B. subtilis without the pgsA gene [2].

Media Optimization [1]

Of the various media mentioned above as taken from the paper, Medium E produced the highest yield:

Medium E was composed of:

• 20 g/l L-glutamic acid
• 12 g/l citric acid
• 80 g/l glycerol
• 7 g/l NH4Cl
• 0.5 g/l MgSO4· 7H2O
• 0.04 g/l FeCl3· 6H2O
• 0.5 g/l K2HPO4
• 0.15 g/l CaCl3· 2H2O.

All of the above are subject to change. The team is also looking into further ways to employ a plasmid to produce a repressor to prevent the degradase gene.
